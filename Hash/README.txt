This is my full implementation of our hash table assignment. This measures the performance of several different access patterns all run through different implementations to compare the best times.

Full Report:
The first thing we can notice about our hash table is that our linear probing is our worst variation by far. While the no collision run through the linear probing method gives us a data point 100,214 microseconds which is slightly faster than most of our other methods. It is when we get to the medium and high collision metrics that we see where the drop off is in overall efficiency. High collision takes 2011731 microseconds. We learned about primary clustering in class which is why we get these massive numbers from linear probing. This clumping causes the O(1) time to turn into an O(n) case which ruins the point of our hash tables.

Our first three methods (Vectors, lists, AVLs) keep a very consistent pattern when collisions happen as they store the collisions elsewhere to prevent the clustering we get in linear probing. Vectors perform well but are slower than lists (126096 vs 79216 microseconds for high collision) because more reallocations are more likely with vectors which will increase our time in the long run, especially when all of these structures are limited to a max length of 6 which plays a huge factor in total time and performance of these structures. Our AVLs are the slowest of the three despite being faster than the vectors for the high collision trial. While AVL search provides a very nice time complexity, they are fundamentally worse especially with our max length being at what it is. There is also the factor that we have rotations and balancing and other functions that our AVLs need to keep that great search time. Lists hold our fastest times in all three categories (108051, 96132, 79216). Our max length of 6 benefits this structure the most as our lists fit into a single cache line meaning we don’t need to worry about missing pointers. Additionally, allocating new nodes is faster than our vectors, meaning these are the kings of chaining.

The last two methods (Quadratic Probing and Double Hashing) are interesting cases. Starting with double hashing, we have decent numbers for our no collision (149502) and high collision (148759) data sets, but we have a massive outlier for our low collision numbers. For our timing for double hashing with low collision, we have 1001993 microseconds. I personally believe that this is a mistake in my programming and is not what the value is supposed to be. This was likely caused by an issue with how the second hash function handled one of our calculations for our step size or something like the second hash function returning zero. The quadratic function on the other hand is incredibly fast and is the fastest of the three open addressing methods (93359, 91529, 100830 for none, low, and high respectively). This is better than both leaner and double hashing methods because of the way that quadratic probing gets rid of the clustering which allows it to stay incredibly stable within the high collision scenarios while retaining performance in the lower collision scenarios.

Overall, quadratic probing is the most consistent option for a hash function. Quadratic retains a good speed while handling cache efficiency very well. Even though other implementations like our list may have a better overhead, it can quickly slow down due to collisions that can occur with the right data. Linear probing is our worst implementation and will most likely never be used within a business context.
